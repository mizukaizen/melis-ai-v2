Graham wrote the essay in July 2013, partway through his time running Y Combinator. By that point he had funded several hundred startups and watched a recognisable pattern: the cohort arriving in Mountain View kept asking the wrong question. They wanted to know how their startup would scale. Graham's answer was that the question was premature — scaling could only happen after a working manual version existed, and the manual version was almost always something the founders found beneath them.

The essay is a catalogue of the unscalable moves that worked. The Collison brothers personally installing Stripe on the laptops of their first users. The Airbnb founders going door to door in New York to photograph listings. Steve Wozniak hand-soldering the first Apple computers. The Pinterest team manually messaging early users to coax them back. Each example carries the same underlying lesson: the founders did the thing themselves, slowly, badly at first, and the act of doing it gave them the information that no analytics dashboard could.

The essay's afterlife is YC's "do things that don't scale" mantra, repeated to every incoming batch since. Like most mantras, it is now overused as a slogan and underused as an instruction. The original essay is short, specific, and worth re-reading at the start of any new venture — particularly the cases where the founder is sure they are too senior for this kind of work.
