<p>Four authors — Rick Levine, Christopher Locke, Doc Searls, David Weinberger — posted the document to a single web page on 1 April 1999. It was free, plain-text, and went viral inside a fortnight. The book-length version followed in 2000. The original page is still up.</p>

<p>The argument is structural. The internet had reconnected people to people, in public, at zero cost. The corporations of the late 1990s were still operating as if the only legitimate channel was a press release and the only legitimate voice was a CEO's. The manifesto called this a category error. The conversations were going to happen anyway; companies could either join them or be talked about without being present.</p>

<p>Twenty-five years later, almost every thesis has either come true (markets are conversations) or been so completely absorbed that it sounds tautological. The most interesting move now is to re-read the document with the recent AI shift in mind — what does it mean when half of the conversation, on both sides, is being generated by language models?</p>
