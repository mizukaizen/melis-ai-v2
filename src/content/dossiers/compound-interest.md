Jacob Bernoulli first studied continuous compounding in 1683, deriving the constant now called e in the process. The mathematical structure has been understood for three and a half centuries. The financial industry has used compounding to price bonds and loans for almost as long. The principle is one of the few in finance with no genuine controversy attached.

What is contested is the metaphor's reach. The financial mathematics is exact; the metaphor — that everything worthwhile in life compounds — is approximate. The metaphor has been carried furthest by Charlie Munger, who returns to it repeatedly in talks and letters, and by Warren Buffett, whose entire investment record is one long demonstration of what holding good assets for fifty years does. Buffett's own framing is plainer than Bernoulli's: the secret is not finding the wonder; it is sitting still after finding it.

The harder claim is that compounding applies to non-financial domains. Knowledge compounds: the polymath at fifty knows things the same person could not have known at twenty even with full-time study, because each new piece anchors to a larger lattice. Reputation compounds: small acts of competence, repeated, accrete into the trust that opens doors no résumé can. Fitness, language fluency, technical skill, friendship — all show the same shape. The shape is not metaphor. The mathematics is the same. The mistake is to imagine the financial case is the most important one.
