<p>Meadows was one of the lead authors of the 1972 Limits to Growth report — the first widely-read systems-dynamics modelling of global resource use. She wrote Thinking in Systems as a teaching draft in the 1990s. It sat in a drawer; she died in 2001; her colleagues published it in 2008. It is the cleanest distillation of the discipline she helped found.</p>

<p>The vocabulary is small and exact. A stock is a measurable quantity (water in a bathtub, money in an account, agents in a system). A flow is what changes the stock (the tap, the drain, the hiring rate). A feedback loop is when an output of the system becomes one of its own inputs — and feedback loops are either reinforcing (the rich get richer) or balancing (the thermostat). Everything else in the book is the consequence of those three words composed together.</p>

<p>The most-quoted chapter is "Places to Intervene in a System" — a hierarchy of leverage points, ordered from least to most powerful. Numbers (taxes, prices) are the weakest lever. Rules of the system are stronger. Goals are stronger still. The paradigm out of which the system was built is the strongest. The bug, Meadows notes, is that almost everyone intervenes at the weakest layer.</p>
