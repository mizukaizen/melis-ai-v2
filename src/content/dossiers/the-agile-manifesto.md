The seventeen signatories met at the Snowbird ski resort in Utah from 11 to 13 February 2001. They had come from competing methodological camps — Extreme Programming, Scrum, DSDM, Crystal, Adaptive Software Development, Feature-Driven Development, Pragmatic Programming — and the meeting was an attempt to find what their approaches had in common rather than what divided them. The document that emerged was deliberately short. The full text, including the four values and twelve principles, runs to under five hundred words.

The form of the manifesto matters. Each of the four values is framed as a comparison rather than an absolute. "Individuals and interactions over processes and tools" is followed by the explicit clause "that is, while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more". The manifesto refuses the binary that its later adopters often impose. It says the items on the right have value; it ranks them second.

The aftermath is complicated. Agile spread quickly through software organisations in the 2000s and into enterprise IT in the 2010s. By the 2020s it had become a billion-dollar industry of certifications, frameworks (SAFe, LeSS, DAD, Disciplined Agile), and consultancies — almost all of which have been criticised, often by the original signatories, for inverting the manifesto's intent. The Snowbird seventeen are split on the legacy. Some believe the spread was the point; others believe the corruption was inevitable; a few have written explicit retractions. The original document, unchanged on a single web page since 2001, remains the cleanest available statement of what the movement was for.
