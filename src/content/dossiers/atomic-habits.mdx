<p>Clear stitched the book together from a decade of newsletter writing, boiling behavioural research down into one operating frame: habits are compound interest applied to behaviour. A one per cent improvement, kept up, becomes a thirty-seven-times improvement over a year. Same maths Buffett uses on capital — just applied to whether you went for the run.</p>

<p>The mechanical core is the habit loop — cue, craving, response, reward — and four laws to engineer each step: make it obvious, make it attractive, make it easy, make it satisfying. The loop isn't original. What is original is the discipline of writing each chapter as one small lever you can actually pull tomorrow morning, not a Big Idea you'll forget by Friday.</p>

<p>The deeper move sits underneath all the tactics. Clear argues every action is a vote for the kind of person you're becoming. The goal isn't to read a book; the goal is to become a reader. That shift — from outcome-thinking to identity-thinking — is the part that stays useful long after the chapter highlights have faded.</p>
