Searle published the experiment in the journal Behavioral and Brain Sciences in 1980, at a moment when symbolic artificial intelligence — programmes that manipulated explicit logical representations — was the dominant research programme in the field. The paper appeared with twenty-eight commentaries from leading philosophers and AI researchers, all attacking Searle from different angles. The exchange ran longer than the original paper and is one of the founding documents of philosophy of mind as a modern discipline.

The setup is plain. A person who speaks no Chinese sits in a closed room. Chinese characters are passed in through a slot. The person consults an enormous rule book — written in English — that says, in effect, "when this sequence comes in, send that sequence out". The output is, by stipulation, fluent Chinese. To a Chinese speaker outside the room, the room appears to understand Chinese. Searle's claim: the person inside understands nothing, and adding more rule book does not change that. Therefore, no programme of symbol manipulation is sufficient for understanding.

The experiment has been attacked, defended, and refined for forty-five years. The systems reply, the robot reply, the brain simulator reply — each pulls at a different premise. Searle has answered each in turn. None of the replies are obviously right; the experiment is not obviously right either. What is durable is the framing: the question of whether processing produces understanding is not the same as the question of whether processing produces correct outputs. The modern LLM era has made the distinction unusually urgent.
