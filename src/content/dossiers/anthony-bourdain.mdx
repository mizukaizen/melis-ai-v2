<p>Bourdain wrote Kitchen Confidential in 2000 — an unguarded backstage account of the restaurant industry that he assumed would end his career, and which instead started a second one. He spent the next eighteen years on television, ostensibly making food shows, actually making something stranger and more durable: a long-form documentary practice that treated meals as the most honest possible interview.</p>

<p>The shows worked because Bourdain refused to be the central character. He let people speak. He listened harder than the camera. He went where the cookery-school graduates would not go — the back rooms, the family kitchens, the bombed-out neighbourhoods where the rice was still being cooked. The result is the closest thing television has produced to genuine ethnography.</p>

<p>He took his own life on 8 June 2018, in a hotel room in France, halfway through filming. The shock was sharp and global, partly because the work had taught a generation of viewers that the world was, on balance, a place that returned curiosity with kindness.</p>
