<p>Frankfurt was a moral philosopher at Princeton. The essay began as a faculty talk in 1986, circulated as a samizdat photocopy for two decades, and was finally published as a small hardback in 2005 — at which point it spent half a year on the New York Times bestseller list and was read by people who never read philosophy.</p>

<p>The argument is precise. A liar knows the truth and chooses to misrepresent it; the liar stands in a relationship of opposition to the truth. The bullshitter does not care about the truth either way. The bullshitter says whatever is useful to the moment — to seem credible, to seem competent, to seem warm. Truth and falsehood are both incidental.</p>

<p>Frankfurt's claim is that this indifference is what makes bullshit uniquely dangerous. The liar is still operating inside a truth-respecting framework. The bullshitter has abandoned the framework entirely. Mass-scale bullshit — politics, advertising, much of corporate communication — corrodes a culture's relationship with truth in a way that lying alone cannot.</p>
