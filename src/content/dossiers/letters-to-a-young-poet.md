Franz Xaver Kappus was a nineteen-year-old cadet at the Theresian Military Academy in Wiener Neustadt in 1902, hesitating between a military career and a literary one. He had read Rilke's early poems and, on discovering that Rilke had attended the same academy a decade earlier, sent him a packet of his own verse with a request for an opinion. Rilke was twenty-seven, recently married, working in Paris as Rodin's secretary, and would not become the major poet of the Duino Elegies for another decade.

He wrote back. The ten surviving letters span 1903 to 1908, addressed from Paris, Rome, Sweden, and Worpswede. The advice is consistently the same — turn inward, ask whether you must write, protect solitude, distrust external judgement, love the difficulty of the questions you have not yet resolved. The letters were not, in Rilke's mind, a treatise; they were the responses of one young man to a younger one. Kappus published them after Rilke's death in 1929, with a brief introduction explaining the context.

The slim book has sold continuously for nearly a century in dozens of languages, mostly read by people in their twenties facing the same choice Kappus faced. The letters have become a standard gift between artists, between parents and creative children, between mentors and students. They survive not because the advice is novel but because the voice is steady and the addressee is anyone willing to imagine themselves into the role. The reader becomes Kappus while reading; the change of position is the point.
