Andy Grove was born András Gróf in Budapest in 1936, survived the Nazi occupation as a child, fled Hungary after the 1956 uprising, and arrived in New York with little English and no money. He earned a chemical engineering doctorate at Berkeley and joined Fairchild Semiconductor, then followed Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore to found Intel in 1968. By the time he wrote High Output Management he was Intel's president, on the way to becoming CEO and Time's Person of the Year.

The book is the management text written by the engineer who arrived at management reluctantly. Grove's frame is operational throughout — production lines, throughput, leading indicators, capacity planning. He treats a manager's job the way an engineer treats a process: identify the high-leverage points, instrument them, and optimise. The tone is neither inspirational nor anecdotal. It is closer to a manual.

The book's modern afterlife is the OKR system, which Grove invented at Intel and which spread through Silicon Valley via John Doerr, his protégé, and the companies Doerr later funded — Google chief among them. The OKR framework is the part most people inherit; the underlying philosophy is older and more important. Most operators who reach for OKRs without reading Grove ship the form without the substance.
