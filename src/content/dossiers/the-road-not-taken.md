Frost wrote the poem in 1915, the year he returned to the United States from a three-year stay in England. The companion of those English walks was the poet Edward Thomas, who had a habit of hesitating at forks in country paths and then sighing over whichever he chose. Frost composed the poem partly as a gentle mockery of Thomas's habit and sent it to him. Thomas, characteristically, missed the joke and read it earnestly. So has everyone since.

The poem appeared as the opening piece of Mountain Interval in 1916. It is sixteen lines of straightforward iambic tetrameter, divided into four stanzas. The narrator, walking in a wood, comes to a fork. He considers both paths, takes one, knowing he can never return for the other. The third stanza is the careful one: he claims to have taken the less-trodden path, then immediately corrects himself — the paths were equally worn. The final stanza is where the misreading happens. The narrator imagines telling the story years later, "with a sigh", saying he took the road less travelled. The sigh and the corrected memory together form the poem's actual argument: the narrator already knows he will tell this lie about himself.

The poem has been read, for a century, as if the final stanza were a triumphant claim about courage. It is not. The text is precise. Frost was repeatedly asked about the poem and repeatedly explained that it was misunderstood. He stopped explaining. The popular reading is now too entrenched to be displaced, and the poem has a kind of double life — one in literary scholarship as a study of self-narration, and one in graduation cards and motivational posters as the opposite of what it actually says. Both lives, somehow, persist.
