Naval Ravikant founded AngelList in 2010 and spent the following decade as one of the most consequential individual angel investors of the smartphone era. He was also, almost as a side effect, the most-quoted Silicon Valley figure on Twitter — short, dense, deliberately aphoristic posts on wealth, philosophy, and the texture of modern work.

The Almanack is not written by Naval. Eric Jorgenson, a young product manager, spent two years editing tweets, podcasts, and interview transcripts into a single book-length argument and released it free online in 2020. Naval approved the project but did not write it. The book is therefore a curation of a body of work rather than an original text — which suits the material, since Naval's contribution was never a single argument but a vocabulary.

The vocabulary is what survives. Specific knowledge. Permissionless leverage. Long-term games with long-term people. Wealth as assets that earn while you sleep. The terms are simple enough to be operational; together they form a way of thinking about working life that is closer to a small philosophy than a self-help book. The reading time is short. The half-life of the ideas is long.
