Turing wrote the paper in 1949 and published it in October 1950 in Mind, the leading British philosophy journal. He was thirty-eight, working at the University of Manchester on the Manchester Mark 1 computer, four years before his conviction for gross indecency and six years before his death. The paper was his only sustained statement of a philosophical position on machine intelligence; the rest of his work on the question is buried in correspondence and unpublished notes.

The paper's structure is unusual for a philosophy journal. Turing opens by replacing the unanswerable question — can machines think — with a behavioural surrogate. A teleprinter conversation between an interrogator and two hidden interlocutors, one human and one machine. If the interrogator cannot reliably tell which is which, the question of whether the machine "thinks" becomes, Turing argues, an empty one. The reframing is the move the field never quite leaves behind. Every modern benchmark for AI capability is, in some sense, a descendant of the imitation game.

The middle section is a sequence of nine objections, named and answered in turn. Some have aged better than others. The mathematical objection (from Gödel's incompleteness) is technical and still contested. The Lady Lovelace objection — that machines can only do what they are programmed to do — has been comprehensively retired by the behaviour of modern neural networks. The consciousness objection remains exactly where Turing left it. The paper does not solve the field's questions; it sets them up in a way that the next seventy-five years of work has been operating inside. The original is twenty-eight pages and reads, even now, with the freshness of a paper written by someone who knows he is opening a field.
