Taleb published Skin in the Game as the fifth book of the Incerto, following Fooled by Randomness, The Black Swan, The Bed of Procrustes, and Antifragile. The sequence is one extended argument about uncertainty; Skin in the Game is the ethical instalment. It is the book where Taleb stops describing the world and starts naming the people he holds responsible for misreading it.

The argument is a single principle applied across domains. Risk-takers and risk-shifters behave differently and reliably reach different conclusions. The bond trader whose own capital is at stake, the surgeon performing the operation, the founder building the company, the soldier in the unit — these people have skin in the game. The columnist, the consultant, the bureaucrat, the policy adviser, the lifestyle guru, and most of academia do not. The structural problem is that the second group is in charge of the institutions that supposedly correct the first.

Taleb is polemical in a way that puts off some readers and recruits others; that is by design. The book's substance is older than the polemic — Hammurabi's code, the medieval guilds, the Roman engineer who slept under the bridge he built. The modern world has slowly engineered the asymmetry out of most professional roles, and the costs of that engineering, Taleb argues, are now compounding.
