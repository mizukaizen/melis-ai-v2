Hamming gave the talk on 7 March 1986 at Bell Labs, where he had spent thirty years working alongside Claude Shannon, John Tukey, and a long list of the people who built modern computing. He was sixty-eight at the time and effectively retired from research; he had moved into teaching at the Naval Postgraduate School in Monterey. The talk was internal, recorded on video, and circulated on photocopy for a decade before being widely posted online.

The structure is autobiographical and confrontational by turns. Hamming describes his own choices, names colleagues by name, and then turns the lens on the audience. Why are you not doing first-rate work? The answers Hamming offers are unflattering. Most researchers spend their careers on problems they did not choose, in fields they did not pick, with effort they assume is fixed but is actually elastic. The great ones, he argues, made conscious choices early about what to work on and conscious choices daily about how much to commit.

The talk's afterlife is large. It is required reading in many computer science PhD programmes, has been quoted in Y Combinator essays for two decades, and is the single most-cited piece of advice on research careers in the postwar literature. Most of the modern derivatives are softer than the original. The original is worth reading in its raw form precisely because Hamming refuses to soften.
