Munger was born in Omaha in 1924, two doors down from where Buffett would later grow up — though they didn't actually meet until 1959. He served as a meteorologist in the US Army Air Corps during World War II, did law at Harvard without an undergrad degree, and practised law in LA until the late 1960s, when his own investment returns made the day job redundant. He joined Berkshire Hathaway officially in 1978 as vice-chairman and stayed there until he died in November 2023, aged ninety-nine.

For most of his career he was the quiet one. Buffett did the talking; Munger did the dry one-liners. The Munger most people quote — the latticework speech, the Harvard School commencement, the USC Law School talk, the Psychology of Human Misjudgement — is almost all from the 1990s onwards. By the 2000s he was the philosophical anchor at every Berkshire annual meeting, dropping single sentences that often outlasted Buffett's much longer answers.

What Munger's actually known for is the multidisciplinary move. His view: the world's hardest problems sit across disciplines, and the person with one model — finance, law, engineering, psych — gets blindsided by everything they ignored. The fix is the latticework: a working command of the big ideas from a dozen major fields, all running at once. The idea isn't original; polymaths existed before him. But he turned it into a programme any serious operator could actually follow. Most don't. The few who do, he reckoned, are obvious from a mile off.
