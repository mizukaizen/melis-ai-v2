Munger was born in Omaha in 1924, two doors down from where Warren Buffett would later grow up — though they did not meet until 1959. He served in the US Army Air Corps in World War II, trained as a meteorologist, studied law at Harvard without an undergraduate degree, and practised law in Los Angeles until the late 1960s, when investment returns from Munger's own partnership made law unnecessary. He joined Buffett at Berkshire Hathaway in 1978 and remained vice-chairman until his death in November 2023, at the age of ninety-nine.

The public Munger emerges late. For the first three decades of the Berkshire partnership he was the silent half — Buffett the voluble, Munger the dry interlocutor. The Munger of the latticework speeches, the Harvard School commencement, the USC Law School talks, the Psychology of Human Misjudgement, is mostly a figure of the 1990s onward. By the early 2000s he was the philosophical anchor of the annual Berkshire meetings, dispensing one-line judgements that often outlasted Buffett's longer answers.

Munger's intellectual legacy is the multidisciplinary stance. The contention is that the world's important problems cut across disciplines, and that the person with one model — finance, law, engineering, psychology — will be blindsided by the considerations sitting in the disciplines they have ignored. The remedy is the latticework: a working command of the big ideas from a dozen major fields, deployed together. The framework is not original — polymaths have always existed — but Munger turned it into a programme that any serious operator could, in principle, follow. Most do not. The few who do, he claimed, are recognisable from a long way off.
