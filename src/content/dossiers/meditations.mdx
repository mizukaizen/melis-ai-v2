<p>The text was written in Greek, in private, during the last decade of Aurelius' life. He was emperor from 161 to 180 AD — the closing decade of the Pax Romana, the plague years, the campaigns on the Danube. He had no readers in mind. The book exists because someone, after his death, refused to destroy the notebooks.</p>

<p>The structure is not a structure. It is twelve books of fragments — instructions to self, reminders, arguments with one's own laziness. The same themes return: do the work, accept what cannot be controlled, remember death, be kind, be honest, be useful.</p>

<p>Stoic philosophy was the operating system of the late Republican and early Imperial Roman elite. Aurelius is its highest exemplar — not because his ideas are original (they are mostly Epictetus and Seneca), but because the doctrine is being applied in real conditions of unlimited power. The notebooks are a refusal to be ruined by that power.</p>
