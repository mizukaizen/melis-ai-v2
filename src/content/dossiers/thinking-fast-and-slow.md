Kahneman shared the 2002 Nobel in Economics with Vernon Smith for the work he did with the late Amos Tversky in the 1970s — work that founded the field now called behavioural economics. Thinking, Fast and Slow is the popular-audience consolidation, written when he was seventy-seven, of a career spent demonstrating that the rational-actor model of the human mind is wrong in specific, predictable ways.

The book is structured as a tour of those ways. Each chapter takes a single cognitive bias — anchoring, availability, framing, the planning fallacy, regression to the mean — and shows it operating in laboratory data, in everyday judgement, and in the high-stakes professional judgement of doctors, judges, and investors. Kahneman is careful not to oversell. Most of the biases catch him out too, he reports, even after a lifetime of cataloguing them.

The deeper move is the two-systems frame. System 1 is fast, parallel, automatic, and good enough for most situations. System 2 is slow, serial, effortful, and the part the reader identifies with as "me". The trouble is that System 2 is lazy. It rubber-stamps almost everything System 1 produces. The book's argument is not that System 1 is bad — it is that the division of labour between the two systems is invisible to the person doing the thinking.
