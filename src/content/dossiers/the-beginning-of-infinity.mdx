<p>Deutsch is a quantum-computing pioneer at Oxford who decided, midway through his career, that the more interesting question was philosophical: why does progress happen at all, and why did it almost never happen for most of human history? The Beginning of Infinity is the book-length answer.</p>

<p>The argument turns on the concept of a "good explanation" — one that is "hard to vary" while still accounting for the phenomenon. Bad explanations (myth, dogma, just-so stories) can be tweaked to fit any new evidence without losing their shape. Good explanations, by contrast, are tightly constrained — change one part and they break. That tightness is what gives them reach: a good explanation about one thing turns out to apply to many other things.</p>

<p>The Enlightenment, in Deutsch's reading, was the moment a particular cultural stance crystallised — the willingness to seek good explanations, accept correction, and recognise that knowledge is fallible but improvable. He argues this is not a small historical detail but a phase change in the cosmos: the only known mechanism that produces unbounded, open-ended progress. Hence "the beginning of infinity."</p>

<p>The book is sprawling — chapters on quantum mechanics, evolution, voting systems, aesthetics, the moral status of memes. Some of it lands; some of it does not. The core is the explanation theory, and it is one of the strongest pieces of philosophical writing this century.</p>
