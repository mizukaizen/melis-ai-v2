<p>Harari wrote Sapiens as an Israeli historian writing for his own students. The book's structure is the structure of three "revolutions" — the Cognitive Revolution roughly 70,000 years ago, the Agricultural Revolution roughly 12,000 years ago, the Scientific Revolution roughly 500 years ago — with the Industrial and the in-progress Biotech/AI revolutions as continuations.</p>

<p>The central claim is unsexy when stated plainly and breathtaking when followed through. Other animals cooperate in small numbers; humans cooperate in millions because they share fictions — gods, nations, money, corporations, human rights. None of these things exist outside human minds, and all of them are load-bearing infrastructure for the world they inhabit. The capacity to imagine, agree on, and live by collective fictions is what made the cognitive revolution a revolution.</p>

<p>The book has been read by something like thirty million people, which is its own data point about how powerful the frame is. The professional historians' critique is fair: Harari simplifies, sometimes wildly. The point of the book is not academic precision; the point is to give a non-specialist reader the macro-frame inside which their daily life sits. Most readers will never have had one. Once they do, it cannot be un-had.</p>
