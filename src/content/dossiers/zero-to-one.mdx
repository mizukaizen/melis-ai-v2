<p>The book is built out of the notes Blake Masters took in Thiel's 2012 Stanford class on startups. Thiel revised those notes into a short, deliberately provocative volume. It came out in 2014 and has stayed in print because the central argument has not aged.</p>

<p>The structure of the book is the structure of the argument. Competition is for losers. Monopoly is the prize, but successful monopolies hide what they are — Google pretends to be one search engine among many; an in-flight restaurant pretends it is in a competitive market and goes broke. The contrarian question — what important truth do very few people agree with you on — is the test for whether a founder is even working on the right problem.</p>

<p>Underneath the provocations is a quieter claim about the shape of the future. Thiel divides views of the future on two axes: definite versus indefinite, optimistic versus pessimistic. He argues the rich-world default has slid into indefinite optimism — the belief that things will get better but no one in particular needs a plan. The book is a long argument for definite optimism: somebody has to build the thing, and that somebody has to know what they are building.</p>
