Feynman was born in Queens in 1918 to a Lithuanian Jewish family. He arrived at Princeton in 1939 to begin doctoral work under John Wheeler. By 1943 he was at Los Alamos, in his mid-twenties, calculating the critical mass for the first plutonium device and amusing himself by picking the locks on filing cabinets containing the most classified documents in the United States. The pattern of the rest of his life is already visible in those years — first-rate technical work alongside an irrepressible mischievousness.

The Nobel arrived in 1965 for the path-integral formulation of quantum electrodynamics — the standard way the calculation has been done since. Feynman shared it with Schwinger and Tomonaga. The technical work, which most people who have heard of Feynman have never read, is dense and original; the public Feynman, who emerges in the 1970s, is the one most people know. Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman, the 1985 collection of anecdotes, sold in numbers that books by Nobel physicists do not normally reach.

The figure that survives is partly the work and partly the manner. The 1986 Challenger commission, where Feynman dipped a piece of O-ring rubber into a glass of iced water on live television to demonstrate why the shuttle had exploded, captured the temperament in one move. Two years later he was dead of cancer. The work and the writings have remained more present in their fields than almost any contemporary's — partly because the work is enduring, mostly because the manner makes the work approachable in a way most physics is not.
