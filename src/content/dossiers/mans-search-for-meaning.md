Frankl was a Viennese psychiatrist, already developing his theory of meaning-centred therapy when the Nazis annexed Austria in 1938. He, his wife, his parents, and his brother were deported in 1942. Only Frankl and one sister survived. He wrote the book in nine days in 1945, dictating it to three secretaries; the first edition appeared in German in 1946 under a title that translates as A Psychologist Experiences the Concentration Camp.

The book is in two halves. The first is a memoir of the camps — restrained, clinical, written by a doctor observing his fellow inmates as much as himself. The second is the theory, logotherapy, that Frankl was already developing before the war and that the camp experience tested in the most extreme possible conditions. The theory's claim is that the primary human drive is not pleasure (Freud) or power (Adler) but meaning — the need to perceive a reason for one's existence.

Frankl was clear, throughout the rest of his career, that the camp experience did not prove his theory. He had already arrived at it; the camps simply did not falsify it. That distinction matters. Survival was overwhelmingly a matter of luck — selection, illness, the timing of liberation. But Frankl insisted that the inner life of those who endured longer than they had any biological right to was characterised, almost without exception, by the presence of a future they were holding out for: a person to find, a manuscript to finish, a child to see again.
