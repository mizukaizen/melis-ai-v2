Eudaimonia is the central concept of Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics, the lecture notes from his teaching at the Lyceum in Athens around 350 BC. The word combines eu (good, well) and daimon (spirit, guiding genius) — literally, "having a good spirit". The most common modern translation, "happiness", is universally regarded by classicists as a serious mistranslation; "flourishing" or "human flourishing" is closer, though still imperfect. Many scholarly editions now leave the word in Greek to preserve its strangeness.

Aristotle's argument is that eudaimonia is the highest human good — the thing pursued for its own sake, not for the sake of anything else. Pleasure, honour, wealth, health, and the rest are all instrumentally valuable; eudaimonia is the only good chosen for itself. The argument proceeds by elimination. Pleasure is too transient; honour depends on others; wealth is a means, not an end. What remains is the activity of the soul in accordance with virtue, sustained across the length of a complete life. That is eudaimonia, and Aristotle spends the next nine books of the Ethics working out what it requires.

The concept's afterlife is wider than its original setting. The Stoics adopted and transformed it. Medieval Christian ethics absorbed it into the doctrine of beatitude. The modern positive-psychology movement has tried, with mixed success, to operationalise it. Most recently, the philosopher Martha Nussbaum and the economist Amartya Sen have built capabilities-based theories of human development around a recognisably Aristotelian frame. The word resists modernisation in part because it pre-dates the distinction between subjective wellbeing and objective flourishing that modern English wants to draw. Aristotle thought they were the same thing, properly understood. The modern reader who finds this strange is finding the right thing strange.
