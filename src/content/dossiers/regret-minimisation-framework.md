Bezos was thirty years old, working as a senior vice president at D.E. Shaw in 1994 when he read the statistic that web usage was growing at 2,300 per cent a year. He drafted a list of twenty product categories that could be sold online and chose books — the one with the largest catalogue, the lowest unit cost, and the smallest shipping risk. The decision to leave a lucrative Wall Street job in mid-career was made, by his own account, using the framework he later described in interviews.

The framework's mechanic is simple. Imagine yourself at eighty, looking back. Which decision will produce more regret — taking the leap and possibly failing, or staying put and watching the opportunity pass? Bezos found, when he ran the exercise on himself, that the regret of not trying was certain to dominate the regret of trying and failing. The framework gave the answer before any of the financial modelling did.

The framework has since been adopted as standard founder vocabulary, but the original is more careful than the popular version. Bezos applies it only to the irreversible — the one-way doors, in his later terminology. Most decisions, he is at pains to point out, are two-way doors and should not be agonised over at all. The regret-minimisation calculus is reserved for the small handful of decisions that genuinely cannot be undone, and the discipline is partly knowing which decisions those are.
