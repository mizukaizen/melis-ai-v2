<p>Jobs delivered the address at the Stanford University commencement on 12 June 2005. He was fifty years old. The pancreatic cancer that would kill him in 2011 had been diagnosed eighteen months earlier; the first surgery had been ten months earlier. The audience did not know.</p>

<p>The speech is three stories, told in order. The first is about dropping out of Reed College and how a calligraphy class he sat in on for fun became the typographic foundation of the Mac a decade later — the dots only connect when looked at backwards. The second is about being fired from Apple in 1985, the wilderness years, and the return. The third is about death.</p>

<p>The structure looks artless. It is not. Each story makes a single argument; the three arguments together make a fourth. Most commencement speeches age badly because they were designed to be agreeable. This one ages well because Jobs was, characteristically, not trying to be agreeable. He was trying to say something he believed before he ran out of time to say it.</p>
