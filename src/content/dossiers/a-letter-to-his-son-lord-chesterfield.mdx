<p>Philip Stanhope began writing to his illegitimate son Philip when the boy was five. He wrote almost weekly for the next thirty years, finally collected and published posthumously in 1774 by the son's widow, against the family's objections. The Earl never expected the letters to be read by anyone else, and that intimacy is the source of their force.</p>

<p>The instruction is comprehensive — Latin, French, history, manners, dress, conversation, diplomacy. Modern readers find some of the advice cynical (Chesterfield is teaching the son to navigate eighteenth-century European courts) and some of it astonishingly modern: do not interrupt; do not bore people; do not be the loudest person in the room; do the dull thing well or do not do it.</p>

<p>The book that the letters became was attacked in its own day — Samuel Johnson said it taught "the morals of a whore and the manners of a dancing master" — and has been rediscovered every generation since. The attacks are not wrong; the rediscoveries are not wrong either. Both can be true.</p>
