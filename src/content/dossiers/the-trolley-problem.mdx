<p>Foot introduced the case in a 1967 essay on the doctrine of double effect — the medieval distinction between intended and merely foreseen consequences. Judith Jarvis Thomson extended it in 1976 with the famous "fat man on the footbridge" variant: would you push a heavy stranger off a bridge to stop the trolley?</p>

<p>Most people will pull the lever. Most people will not push the man. The two cases share the same arithmetic — one death prevents five — but feel completely different. The Trolley Problem is the experiment that exposed the gap between consequentialist moral reasoning (the maths) and deontological moral reasoning (the act itself matters).</p>

<p>The case has had a strange second life. It was buried inside academic ethics for thirty years, then resurfaced as the standard test for self-driving car decision-making, then again for AI alignment. The original point — that humans do not actually reason like calculators — turned out to matter operationally.</p>
