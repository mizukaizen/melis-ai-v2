Plutarch records the puzzle in his Life of Theseus around 75 AD, attributing the underlying preservation tradition to the Athenians, who maintained the legendary ship as a public monument for centuries by replacing each plank as it rotted. The philosophical question of whether the preserved object was still the same ship was, Plutarch reports, "a standing example among the philosophers" — meaning it was already old by his time. Some scholars trace the kernel of the question back to Heraclitus and the river one cannot step into twice.

Thomas Hobbes added the now-canonical twist in De Corpore in 1655. If, while the original planks are being gradually replaced, a second carpenter collects the discarded boards and reassembles them into a ship, the puzzle bifurcates. There are now two candidate ships: the maintained-by-replacement one and the rebuilt-from-originals one. Most respondents intuitively side with the maintained ship — but most respondents also struggle to articulate why. The intuition is real; the justification is not.

The puzzle has been revisited by almost every major philosopher of identity since — Locke, Hume, Parfit, and the modern philosophy of mind literature on personal identity over time. The case is not solvable in any final sense because "identity" is not one concept but several. The useful move is not to find the answer but to notice which kind of identity is at stake in any given application of the puzzle, and to be precise about it.
