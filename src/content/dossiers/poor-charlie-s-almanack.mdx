<p>The book is not a book in the conventional sense. Peter Kaufman gathered Munger's major talks — the Harvard School address, the USC Law lecture, the Psychology of Human Misjudgement — alongside Berkshire annual-meeting transcripts, letters, and Munger's own marginalia. The result is a 500-page omnibus that can be read in any order. The recursion of Munger's thinking is the same in every fragment.</p>

<p>The core argument is multidisciplinary. Most professional thinking is the man-with-a-hammer problem — a specialist who reduces every situation to the one model he knows. Munger's answer is to carry a "latticework" of the big ideas from every major discipline: psychology, economics, statistics, physics, biology, history. Lattice them together and most real-world problems become tractable.</p>

<p>Underneath the latticework is a second move: inversion. Munger's famous advice is "invert, always invert" — borrowed from Jacobi. Instead of asking how to succeed, ask what would cause failure, and avoid those things. The compounding effect of doing the obvious things and not blowing up is, Munger claims, the entire game.</p>
