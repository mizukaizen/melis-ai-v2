Kennedy delivered the speech at Rice University in Houston on 12 September 1962, eighteen months into his presidency and fifteen months after he had first committed the country to the lunar goal in a May 1961 address to Congress. By September 1962 the Soviet Union had put two men into orbit; the United States had managed three sub-orbital flights. The technical gap was wide. The political pressure to articulate why the United States was attempting the Moon at all was widening with it.

The Rice speech is the public answer. Kennedy frames the Apollo programme as the latest in a long sequence of human leaps — from cave to plough to ship to aeroplane — compressed into a single decade. He cites the cost ($40 a head per American per year), the technological hurdles (heat shields, navigation, rocket engines as yet untested), and the central rationale: hard goals organise civilisations in ways soft goals do not. The speech runs eighteen minutes and is mostly remembered for one sentence.

The aftermath is the demonstration. NASA's budget tripled. The Apollo programme employed 400,000 people at its peak. The Saturn V rocket, the F-1 engine, the lunar excursion module, the Houston mission control complex — none existed when Kennedy spoke and all were operational by 1969. Kennedy himself was assassinated fourteen months after the speech and never saw the programme reach its target. The target was hit on 20 July 1969, on schedule. The speech remains the textbook example of an ambitious deadline that produced its own capability.
