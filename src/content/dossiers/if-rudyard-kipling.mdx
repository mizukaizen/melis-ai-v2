<p>Kipling wrote the poem in 1910, when his son John was twelve. The boy died in the First World War five years later, at the Battle of Loos. Kipling spent the rest of his life writing about that grief — most of it indirectly, much of it in poems his son never read.</p>

<p>The poem is technically one long conditional sentence. Eight stanzas of "if you can…" — each clause stacking a virtue the speaker hopes his son will manage — culminating in the four-word resolution at the end. The structure means the poem cannot be misread; it is held together by its own grammar.</p>

<p>It became, almost immediately, the most-quoted English-language poem on the discipline of self-mastery. It is engraved on the wall above the players' entrance at Wimbledon Centre Court. It is recited at Sandhurst. It has also been mocked — for jingoism, for Victorian sentimentality, for the line about treating triumph and disaster as imposters. The mockery does not stick. The poem keeps coming back.</p>
