Aristotle gives the earliest extant statement of the method in the Posterior Analytics, around 350 BC. A first principle, in his usage, is a foundational proposition that cannot be deduced from any other — the irreducible starting point of a chain of reasoning. The medieval scholastics inherited the framework; Descartes radicalised it in the Discourse on Method by proposing that all received knowledge should be doubted until rebuilt from indubitable atoms. The methodological lineage is long.

Its modern popularisation comes from Elon Musk, who described it in a 2013 TED interview as the explicit technique by which Tesla brought down battery costs. The accepted wisdom at the time was that lithium-ion batteries cost roughly $600 per kilowatt-hour and could not be cheaper. Musk's first-principles analysis broke the battery down into its raw materials — cobalt, nickel, aluminium, steel, polymer, carbon — and priced them at commodity rates. The materials cost was around $80 per kilowatt-hour. Everything else was packaging, manufacturing, and convention. Tesla spent the next decade closing the gap, and the analyst consensus on battery economics has been catching up since.

The method is harder than it sounds. Most domains have hundreds of conventional assumptions stacked on top of each other, and the operator who tries to question all of them at once produces nothing. The skill is in choosing which assumption to interrogate — usually the one that feels most settled and most central. The other assumptions can be accepted on faith long enough to do the work.
