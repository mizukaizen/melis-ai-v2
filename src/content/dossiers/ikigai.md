Ikigai is a Japanese compound noun written 生き甲斐 — iki, meaning "to live", and gai, an older suffix meaning "worth" or "value". The concept is centuries old in Japanese usage. It is one of a small set of Japanese terms — mono no aware, wabi-sabi, komorebi — that resist clean English translation because the underlying culture has stitched together a slightly different cognitive shape. Most attempts to render it in English lose something.

The modern Japanese understanding of the term traces in large part to Mieko Kamiya, a psychiatrist who published Ikigai ni Tsuite (On the Meaning of Life) in 1966 after years of working with Hansen's disease patients in Tokyo. Kamiya's framework distinguished ikigai-kan, the feeling of having ikigai, from the underlying ikigai itself. The book is rarely cited in English-language popularisations of the concept; it is the closest thing to a foundational text and disagrees with most of what those popularisations claim.

The familiar four-circle Venn diagram — passion, mission, vocation, profession — is not Japanese. It was created by a Spanish astrologer, Andrés Zuzunaga, around 2011 as a "purpose" framework, and was later mislabelled as ikigai by a Western blogger in 2014. The mislabelled version spread widely, especially through the 2016 book Ikigai: The Japanese Secret to a Long and Happy Life by Héctor García and Francesc Miralles, which conflated the concept with research on Okinawan longevity. Most Western readers who think they know ikigai know this hybrid. The original is humbler, more local, and less interested in optimisation. Knowing the difference matters because the original is the more useful concept; the popular version is mostly a productivity framework wearing a Japanese name.
