King delivered the speech on 28 August 1963, on the steps of the Lincoln Memorial, as the closing address of the March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom. The crowd of 250,000 was the largest protest gathering in American history at that point. The speech ran seventeen minutes. It was carried live by all three major networks and by radio across the country. Television was eight years past colour broadcasts; King's audience, in the moment, was tens of millions.

The prepared text drew heavily on King's earlier speeches — the bad cheque metaphor, the references to the Declaration and the Emancipation Proclamation, the framing of the moment as a turning point. The dream passage, the one the speech is named for, was not in the typed draft. Mahalia Jackson, standing behind King on the platform, had heard him deliver a version of the dream sequence in Detroit two months earlier. As King approached the end of the prepared text, she called out: tell them about the dream, Martin. He stepped away from the lectern and spoke from memory.

The improvised section is the section that survives. The earlier passages are forceful but specific to 1963; the dream passage is timeless because it describes a country that does not yet exist. King was thirty-four. He had less than five years to live. The Civil Rights Act passed in July 1964; the Voting Rights Act in August 1965. The chain of legislation that followed did not flow from the speech alone, but the speech was the moment in which the moral case became unanswerable in the public mind, and the legislation became politically possible.
