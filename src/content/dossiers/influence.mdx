<p>Cialdini spent three years undercover — at car dealerships, fundraising calls, telemarketing operations, cult recruiting events — watching the people who get paid to change minds. The first edition came out in 1984. The "New and Expanded" 2021 edition added a seventh principle and updated the research, but the original architecture has held.</p>

<p>The book's contribution is taxonomic. Persuasion is not magic; it is a small set of fixed-action patterns humans run because the patterns are usually correct. Reciprocation: a received gift creates pressure to give back. Social proof: when many others do it, it is assumed to be the right move. Authority: the lab coat is obeyed. Scarcity: dwindling supply increases desire. Liking: similar people say yes to each other. Commitment and consistency: once A is said, B follows to preserve consistency. Unity (the seventh, added 2016): the deepest persuasion is identity-level — "people like us do things like this."</p>

<p>The hard part is not learning the levers. The hard part is noticing them being run on the listener in real time and choosing what to do about it. Cialdini is honest about this: even after forty years of studying the field, he reports that the patterns still catch him out.</p>
