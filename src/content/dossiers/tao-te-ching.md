The Tao Te Ching is traditionally attributed to Laozi, a contemporary of Confucius in the sixth century BC who supposedly composed the book in a single sitting before retiring westward through a mountain pass and disappearing from history. Modern scholarship dates the text closer to the fourth century BC and considers it the product of a school rather than a single author. The dispute does not affect the reading; the text behaves like a singular voice.

The structure is unstructured. Eighty-one short chapters, none more than a page, none arranged in any obvious sequence. Themes recur — water, the uncarved block, the female principle, the absence of striving — and the chapters themselves are layered with paradox in a way that resists single readings. The text is meant to be circled back to, not finished. There are over two hundred English translations; no two read alike, and the discrepancies are themselves part of the experience.

The book's influence is hard to overstate. Taoism, Chan Buddhism, Zen, much of Chinese landscape painting and poetry, and a great deal of East Asian political philosophy descend from it. In the West, the text was largely unknown until the nineteenth century and then absorbed in scattered ways — by Tolstoy, by Heidegger, by the counterculture of the 1960s, by the management thinkers of the 1980s who recognised in wu wei a corrective to the cult of total control. Each generation finds the part it needs.
