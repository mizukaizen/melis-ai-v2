<p>I have run more Claude sessions than is reasonable for any one person — across Cowork, Code, the API, agents in production. Across all of them, one thing keeps showing up: the quality of the session is decided almost entirely in the first three minutes.</p>

<p>Not the prompt. Not the model. Not the tools. The <em>setup</em> — the spine of context, intent, and constraint that you lay down before you ask Claude to do anything.</p>

<p>Most operators skip this. They type the request, hit enter, and then spend the next forty minutes correcting the drift they could have prevented with a single paragraph at the top.</p>

<h2>The <em>three-minute spine</em></h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Minute one — Who am I and what am I doing.</strong> One paragraph. Role, current state, what I am trying to ship today. Not "I am a founder" — "I am the founder of a 4-person team shipping a SaaS for SMB lawyers, currently rebuilding the onboarding flow because trial-to-paid conversion is stuck at 4%."</li>
  <li><strong>Minute two — The shape of the answer I want.</strong> Format, length, tone, audience. "Bullet list, under 200 words, plain English, for someone non-technical." Or "long-form prose, no headings, written like Paul Graham at his most direct." The model cannot guess this and most defaults are wrong.</li>
  <li><strong>Minute three — What is off-limits and what I have already tried.</strong> The negative space. "Do not suggest I rewrite the homepage — I just did that." "Skip the obvious advice." This single line saves twenty back-and-forths.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote><p>The model is not the bottleneck. Your setup is.</p><cite>A pattern I notice in every session I review</cite></blockquote>

<p>The mistake I made for the first six months was treating Claude like a search engine — fire off the question, react to the result. That is fine for trivial work. For anything load-bearing it is the wrong stance entirely.</p>

<p>The shift was treating the opening as a <em>briefing</em>, not a query. The model is not lazy or stupid; it is genuinely uncertain about what you want. Three minutes of clarity at the front removes the uncertainty and the output changes character — sharper, shorter, more useful.</p>

<p>There is a second-order effect too. The act of writing the briefing forces me to know what I want before I ask. Most days the briefing alone surfaces the answer. The session that follows is faster because the question got better.</p>

<p>None of this is novel. It is the same discipline a good brief gives a designer or a good RFP gives an engineer. The novelty is only that we forgot to apply it to the most capable colleague we have ever hired.</p>
