Most people treat prompting like texting. They fire off a message and hope the model works it out. Then they get mediocre output and conclude that AI is overhyped. It's not overhyped. The prompt was just bad.

There is one structure that works across almost every task. Once you have it, you stop guessing and start getting consistent output. Here it is.

### The four parts

**Role.** Tell the model who it is. Not "you are a helpful assistant" — that's a nothing statement. Be specific: "You are a senior B2B copywriter who specialises in SaaS landing pages. You write for conversion, not admiration."

**Context.** What situation are you in? What does the model need to know to do the job properly? This is where most people go wrong — they assume the model knows things it doesn't. It doesn't know your audience, your tone, your constraints, or your history. Tell it.

**Task.** What do you actually want? One task per prompt. "Write the hero section for my landing page targeting mid-market finance teams who are replacing a legacy tool. Tone: confident, not aggressive. Under 80 words."

**Format.** How do you want the output? Raw HTML, bullet points, a table, plain prose? Specify it. If you don't, the model will pick something that might not fit your workflow.

### Why this works

The structure forces you to think before you type. That's most of the value. A prompt you have to structure is a prompt you have to understand — and when you understand what you're asking for, the model can actually deliver it.

The other benefit is repeatability. If a prompt works, you save it. You iterate on it. You build a library. That's the difference between using AI occasionally and using it operationally.

### The most common mistake

Vagueness at the task level. "Help me with my marketing" is not a task. "Write three subject line options for a win-back email targeting customers who haven't purchased in 90 days, for a direct-to-consumer supplement brand, A/B test friendly" — that's a task.

The more specific your task, the less work the model has to do guessing what you mean, and the more work it can do actually solving the problem.

### One more thing

Examples outperform instructions almost every time. If you want the model to match a tone, show it a piece of writing in that tone. If you want a specific format, give it one example. Your brain is excellent at pattern recognition. So is the model's. Use that.

Start with role, context, task, format. Add examples where you have them. That's it. You don't need a 47-step framework — you need to think clearly about what you're asking for.
