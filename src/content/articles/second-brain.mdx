I've tried every note-taking system. Zettelkasten, PARA, GTD, Things, Notion databases with seventeen properties. Some of them worked for a while. None of them became the durable system I was after. The problem was always retrieval — getting something out was harder than putting it in.

Claude changed that. Not because it's a better filing cabinet — it's not — but because it can actually talk to the information once it's there. Here's how the system works now.

### The structure

I use Obsidian as the storage layer. Plain markdown files, organised in a PARA structure. Nothing exotic. The files sync to my server via Syncthing, which means they're always accessible regardless of device or connection.

Claude is the interface layer. Instead of searching the vault manually, I ask Claude what I want to know. "What did I decide about the pricing model for Lighthouse?" "What's the status of the SWARM build?" "What notes do I have on prediction market strategies?" It reads the relevant files and gives me a coherent answer.

### What actually gets captured

Most second brain systems fail because people try to capture everything. You end up with an enormous archive that's too noisy to be useful. I've narrowed it to three categories:

**Decisions with context.** Not just what I decided, but why. This is the thing I most often want to retrieve — the reasoning behind a choice I made six months ago that I'm now revisiting.

**Patterns and observations.** Things I notice that seem important but don't have a home yet. These often turn into articles, course modules, or product ideas.

**Useful references.** Frameworks I return to, examples I want to keep, research I actually use. Not every interesting thing I read — just the things I can imagine needing again.

### The agent layer

The part that makes this genuinely powerful is that I have agents that write to the vault directly. When something significant happens in a project, the relevant agent captures it. When a bot run completes, the summary goes into the right folder. I'm not manually updating anything — the system updates itself.

That was the missing piece in every previous system I used. Manual capture is a bottleneck. The information you most need to record is often the information you're least likely to write down in the moment.

### The honest limitation

Claude is not magic. If the underlying notes are vague or poorly structured, the output is vague. Garbage in, garbage out applies here as much as anywhere. The system is only as good as the quality of what goes into it.

I spend about ten minutes at the end of each day doing a lightweight review — checking that the important things got captured, filling in anything the agents missed. That's the maintenance cost. It's worth it.

### Where to start

Pick one category of information you consistently struggle to find when you need it. Just one. Build a simple capture habit around that one thing for 30 days. Once it's running, add the next thing.

The temptation is to build the complete system on day one. Resist it. The system you'll actually use is the one you built incrementally, starting from something small that worked.
