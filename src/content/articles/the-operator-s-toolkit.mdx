<p>I keep watching newer operators stack tools the way I once stacked notebooks — accumulating capability without ever using most of it. This essay is about the stack I actually use, the ones I tried and dropped, and the principle that decides which ones stay.</p>

<p>The principle is unfashionable: <em>every tool earns its place by replacing a meaningful unit of work I was already doing.</em> Not by promising to make me more productive. Not by being the new hotness. By replacing work.</p>

<h2>The eleven that <em>stay</em></h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Claude Cowork</strong> — Replaced 2-3 hours of daily desktop work.</li>
  <li><strong>Cursor</strong> — Replaced typing speed and most of pair-programming.</li>
  <li><strong>Notion</strong> — Replaced spreadsheets and most of project management.</li>
  <li><strong>Obsidian</strong> — Replaced second-brain note-taking and is now the substrate for my agents.</li>
</ul>

<blockquote><p>The tools you keep are the ones that replaced something. The ones you drop are the ones that added something.</p><cite>Field note · March 2026</cite></blockquote>

<p>That single sentence is the test I run on every new tool now. If I cannot name what it replaces, it does not survive the trial period.</p>
