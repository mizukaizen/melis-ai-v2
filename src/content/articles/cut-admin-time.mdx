I used to spend about three hours a day on stuff that wasn't the work. Emails. Status updates. Scheduling. Reformatting things. Writing the same kind of message for the fifth time that week. I didn't mind it at first — it felt like productivity. Then I tracked it, and it wasn't.

Over six months I rebuilt how I handle admin. The number came down to under an hour. Here's what actually moved the needle.

### The single biggest win: templating with AI

I have a library of about 40 prompt templates. Each one handles a recurring task — drafting a follow-up email, summarising a meeting, writing a project brief, responding to a sales enquiry. I don't write these from scratch anymore. I run the template, check the output, adjust for anything specific, send.

The key is that the templates carry context. They know my tone, my typical constraints, what I care about. Building them took time upfront. Running them takes seconds.

### Batching beats context switching

I used to handle messages as they came in. This is a trap. Every time you switch from deep work to responding to something, you pay a re-entry cost. For me that cost was about 15 minutes of scattered thinking before I got back into flow.

Now I check messages twice a day, batch-process them with AI assistance, and close the tab. That alone was worth 45 minutes daily.

### Automated first drafts for everything recurring

Weekly updates, monthly reviews, client check-ins — anything that happens on a schedule now has an automated first draft. One of my agents pulls relevant data, formats it against a template, and drops it into my inbox ready to review. I spend ten minutes editing rather than forty minutes writing.

### The things I didn't automate

Relationship-critical communication. Anything where nuance really matters. Creative work I actually enjoy. The goal wasn't to eliminate thinking — it was to eliminate the thinking I was doing poorly because I was tired and rushed.

A lot of admin feels necessary because it's urgent. Most of it isn't actually important. Being ruthless about that distinction is worth more than any tool.

### What I'd do differently

I'd have tracked my time at the start, not three months in. I was guessing about where the time went before I measured it. The measurement was uncomfortable — I was wasting more time than I thought on things I could have automated six months earlier.

Measure first. Then build systems for what you find. That's the actual sequence.
