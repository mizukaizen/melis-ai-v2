In the early 2000s, knowing Excel well was a genuine career advantage. It wasn't universal yet. The person who could build a model, write a VLOOKUP, and handle a pivot table had a real edge over someone who couldn't. Then it became table stakes. You either know Excel or you're explaining why you don't.

AI fluency is at the same inflection point Excel was around 2004. Right now it's still an advantage. In three to five years, it'll be assumed.

### What AI fluency actually means

It doesn't mean knowing how to ask ChatGPT to write a poem. It means knowing how to use AI as a force multiplier on actual work — drafting, research, analysis, automation, decision support. It means knowing when to use it and when not to. It means understanding enough about how these systems work to use them reliably.

The people who will have the hardest time aren't the ones who never tried AI. They're the ones who tried it once, got a mediocre output, and wrote it off. That's like trying Excel once, getting a broken formula, and deciding spreadsheets are useless.

### The gap is bigger than most people realise

There are two very different types of AI user right now. There's the person who opens ChatGPT when they remember it exists, uses it like a search engine, and gets search-engine-quality output. And there's the person who has built workflows, prompt libraries, and agent pipelines — who is running ten times the output they were two years ago.

Both will tell you they "use AI". The gap between them is enormous.

### What actually builds fluency

Daily use. Not occasional experiments — daily use on real work. You need enough reps to develop intuition about what to delegate to the model and what to keep. You need to learn the failure modes so you can spot them. You need to get comfortable iterating on output instead of accepting the first draft.

The fastest way I've seen people develop this is to pick one recurring task — something they do at least three times a week — and commit to doing it with AI assistance for 30 days. Not sometimes. Every time. At the end of 30 days, they know more than most people will learn from a six-hour course.

### The honest caveat

Fluency won't save a bad strategy. AI makes skilled people faster. It also makes confused people more confidently confused. The thinking still has to be good. The AI is a multiplier on whatever you bring to it — which means the quality of what you bring still matters enormously.

But if you're going to invest in a skill right now, and you haven't built real AI fluency yet, that's where I'd start.
