My Story

Eight chapters. The full version.

A working biography. The way it actually went, including the parts that didn't work.

01

The Blueprint

Brisbane, early 2000s — architects for parents, AI in high school

I grew up in Brisbane, the son of a Dutch father and a Chinese-Indonesian mother — both architects. The house was always full of design, drawing, and building things. That early immersion in how things are made — the gap between a rough sketch and something real — never really left me.

Design came first. In grade 12 I was selected to design the cover of the school magazine — not just any edition, but the 50th anniversary issue. Every cover before it had looked like a classic magazine: busy, decorated, packed. I went the other direction entirely. I stripped it back. Clean, minimal, almost nothing on it. I didn't have a name for what I was doing at the time, but I already believed it: that the strongest statement is often the quietest one. That removing things takes more conviction than adding them. It was the first time I understood that restraint is its own kind of confidence — and it's a principle I've carried into everything I've built since.

Outside school I was freelancing inside Australia's esports community — forum signatures, logos, website graphics for gaming teams and players. Nobody paid much, but I was building things people actually used, which mattered more.

Then came a school presentation on AI. I didn't fully understand it yet, but I knew it was the most important thing in the world to understand. That conviction became a through-line I didn't know I was drawing.

Grade 12 AI presentation, Brisbane — the first time I put AI in front of a room.
02

Sharp End of the Spear

QUT 2011–2015 — international competitions, Uber early hire, Shanghai

At QUT I studied Finance and IT simultaneously and threw myself into everything outside the classroom. I ran the Economics and Finance Society — grew it 600%, secured sponsorships from Bloomberg, Deloitte, PwC, and CPA Australia, and won Club of the Year. I competed in international business case competitions across New Zealand, Hong Kong, Bangkok, Montreal, and Sydney — finishing first at multiple. I edited a magazine read by 25,000 students across both campuses.

The most formative experiences were the internships. I joined Uber Queensland as the fourth team member — before the app was mainstream, before anyone knew what Uber was going to become — spending ten months embedded in the launch team, optimising driver onboarding and supply-side growth as the platform expanded across the state. I also spent three months in Shanghai with 3TI Solutions through the CRCC Asia exchange, designing UX for an automotive iPad app. That stint planted something: an early instinct that the world was moving much faster than Brisbane's orbit suggested.

03

The Machine Starts

Deloitte 2016–2017 — RPA pioneer, Singapore and China, Brisbane's first AI research deck

I graduated and joined Deloitte's Technology Consulting practice, where I helped build what would become the firm's Automation & AI practice from the ground up. I became one of Australia's first certified RPA developers and contributed to Deloitte's inaugural proof of concept for a Big 4 Bank. I produced Brisbane's first-ever AI research deck for Senior Partners — at a time when most of them had never heard the term seriously used in a business context.

I was selected for a pioneering AI and automation project at a crown-jewel client in Singapore and China — one of the only Deloitte graduates sent overseas in their first year. I contributed to a multi-million-dollar transformation report recognised by Gartner, Forrester, and IDC. At 22, I was writing AI strategy for some of Australia's largest organisations. The work was extraordinary. It was also 100-hour weeks and, eventually, burnout — and a growing sense that the sheen had worn off. I left at what felt like my peak, with nothing lined up.

04

The Great Unraveling

2017 — the failures, the floor, the reset

What followed was a thorough education in failure. A PowerPoint template store where I spent $1,000 on domain names that turned out to be Microsoft trademarks. Eight simultaneous drop-shipping Instagram accounts that collectively lost money. A crypto fortune I made and then completely destroyed in a run of bad trades that left me genuinely depressed. A final-round interview at a tech giant I was so confident about I'd already flown to the city and started looking at apartments — didn't get it.

I wasn't failing because I lacked ability. I was failing because I hadn't yet found the problem I was built for. Through all of it, one thing kept pulling me back: AI and conversational interfaces.

05

Zero to Agency

bot•hello 2017–2023 — Australia's first conversational AI agency, digital humans, Intercom APAC

In 2017, the idea of an AI-powered customer experience agency was novel enough that most clients needed fifteen minutes just to understand what we were proposing. I launched bot•hello with $720 left in my account. Over six years we delivered 50+ AI and automation projects — Maybelline, Kiehl's, Mercedes-Benz, L'Oréal, Anytime Fitness, the Brisbane Lions.

We built Australia's first retail digital human, for Kiehl's. We became Intercom's first official APAC implementation partner. We partnered with Meta during COVID to help L'Oréal Group clients maintain customer service continuity — work that made the Courier Mail. I was recognised in the Courier Mail's Top 20 Under 40 Entrepreneurs and the B&T 30 Under 30 Awards. I also served as the youngest-ever board member of the Salvation Army Australia, advising on digital strategy — a reminder that the best work goes toward people, not just profit.

Somewhere around year five I noticed I was spending my days helping large companies automate away customer service jobs, and I'd stopped feeling good about it.

bot•hello, Brisbane — co-founding, building, and winning the QUT Bluebox Accelerator Award.
06

The Pivot

2023 — walking away from a profitable business, teaching at scale

So I walked away from a working agency and published a ChatGPT course instead — within months of ChatGPT going public, before the market was saturated, before every educator on the internet had pivoted. Twenty-five thousand people enrolled across 150 countries in the first year. It became the #1 ChatGPT course on Udemy. Revenue hit $250K+ in year one.

That felt different — teaching someone in the Philippines or Nigeria how to use AI to find work, start something, adapt to what's coming. It was the first time in years I went to bed feeling like the work was going to the right place.

07

Back in the Room

PA Consulting 2025 — enterprise AI, Tier 1 banks, agentic transformation

In 2025 I joined PA Consulting in London as AI Agent Innovation Lead and Principal. I built a 63-module AI Academy now deployed across 3,000+ consultants — one of PA's top-rated internal programs. I defined an agentic AI transformation roadmap for a Tier 1 UK bank: value pools, user journeys, new-venture hypotheses, a Horizon 2/3 portfolio framework covering the next five years of AI opportunity.

I led workshops on vibe coding, multi-agent orchestration, AI UX design, and prompt engineering for teams building the next generation of financial services infrastructure. The scale was real. But so was the pull to build my own thing again. I left in late 2025.

Back in the room — London, 2025.
08

The Edge

melis.ai 2026 — London, multi-agent systems, building in the open

Now I'm relaunching melis.ai full-time from London — 73 digital products, a self-hosted OpenClaw multi-agent system running 24/7, Claude Code and Cowork setups with persistent cross-session memory, and a mission I actually believe in: helping people understand and use AI before it happens to them.

I've been at or near the front of every major wave in this space — RPA in 2016, conversational AI in 2017, generative AI in 2023, multi-agent systems now. I don't say that to impress. I say it because I've watched what happens to people caught off guard by each one. I'd rather be the person holding the map.

It's not always tidy. The path here wasn't either. But I'd rather be building at the edge than watching from somewhere safe.

A year in London.

For now I'm in London — soaking it up, taking photos, noticing what people are struggling with, and building things I'd actually want to use myself. I don't have the full picture. But I'm paying attention, and that feels like enough to build from.

09

The Long Bet

2026 and beyond — what's being built, and where it's all heading

The opening quote on this site says everything since high school has been "one long bet that this is the most important thing in the world to understand." This chapter is that bet, made explicit. Right now, from London, I'm rebuilding melis.ai as the thing I always wanted it to be. A new flagship AI course is replacing the original ChatGPT 101 — the one that's already inside 25,000 people's brains across 150 countries. A Skool community for learners who want to go deeper. An async AI build subscription — "AI Builds on Tap" — for founders and operators who want results without the ramp-up time. The audience exists. The infrastructure exists. What's happening now is the assembly.

There's a longer arc behind all of it, called Dreamwith — an AI-led advisory system for entrepreneurs where advisors are spun up around your specific problem, not a generic template. You get three at a time. They know you. They push back. Over time, it merges with the agent infrastructure I've already built — the OpenClaw system, the Claude Code and Cowork tooling, the memory layers — into something that functions less like software and more like a thinking partner that compounds. I think the most important thing AI can do for a person isn't answer their questions. It's know them well enough to ask the right ones.

I don't know exactly how it unfolds from here. The path so far has been too strange to predict. What I do know is that I've been at or near the front of every major wave in this space — and the next one is the biggest yet. If you want to see what's actively in progress — the courses, the tools, the experiments, the things that don't have names yet — The Lab is where it lives.

Thanks for reading. If any of this resonates — or if you're building something and want a thinking partner — you know where to find me.

What Drives Me

Six things I actually believe.

The principles I run on. They show up in the way the work gets made.

Systems over heroics
Build the machine once, then run it. OpenClaw agents, Claude Code workflows, persona networks — compounding value from systems, not headcount.
Leverage, not hustle
One hour of systems work beats one hundred hours of manual effort. That's the whole playbook.
AI for social mobility
AI shouldn't be a corporate privilege. The mission is to close the fluency gap — one student at a time.
Health is the foundation
2017 Wuxi burnout taught me this the hard way. Systems that don't protect the human aren't worth building.
Cinematic sensibility
Beauty and utility aren't opposites. The best systems are also the most elegant. Tarantino would agree.
Honest over impressive
Australian directness. No jargon, no gatekeeping, no hero narratives. Just what actually works.
