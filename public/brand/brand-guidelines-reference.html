00 Manifesto · why we exist

Helping ambitious people win with AI, by treating them as peers — not students.

melis.ai is an operator-led education brand. The work is to take what we're learning at the frontier of AI and pass it down — clearly, honestly, without the certainty most teachers perform. We don't promise outcomes. We point at the thing, describe it accurately, and stop talking. The reader is trusted to decide what to do with it.

Most AI educators are selling certainty in a moment when nobody actually has it. The honest position is that we're all figuring this out — and the difference is whether you're figuring it out by typing prompts in a free chat window or by running a system you've actually built. This brand exists to close that gap. To take the working operation and unbundle it: the principles into essays, the methods into courses, the artefacts into products, the philosophy into a brand book like this one.

The audience is the person already building something — past the basics, past the hype, ready to do the work. We trust them to keep up. We don't dilute. We don't recap. We treat them as a peer, because they are.

"Technology should make people's lives feel lighter, not heavier. Every visual, verbal, and product decision flows from that."

North Star · the test the rest of this document flows from

01 Promise · what we owe the audience

Three things the audience can rely on. Signal. Depth. Restraint.

Most AI educators promise outcomes — money, time, transformation. We don't. The brand promise is narrower and more honest. It's the contract: this is what you'll get from us, no matter the surface.

01 · Promise

Signal, not noise.

λόγος lógos

If we haven't used the tool, technique, or framework in real work — we don't teach it. The library is curated, not generated. No tutorial mill, no SEO churn.

02 · Promise

Operator depth.

φρόνησις phrónēsis

No definitions of what a prompt is. No recaps. We move at the speed of someone already in the work. If you're past the basics, you're the audience.

03 · Promise

Restraint.

σωφροσύνη sōphrosýnē

We don't extend the brand for hype. We don't ship "AI-powered X" because the moment is hot. We don't publish content we wouldn't put our name on in five years. Honest before impressive.

02 Values · what we won't violate

Three commitments. We don't break any of them.

Values are the lines we won't cross even when crossing them would be convenient. Three is enough. Seven is a corporate poster. These are the ones we'd refuse to violate even if a tempting opportunity made one of them inconvenient — and that refusal is the whole point. Each one passes through the North Star below.

North Star · the test the values flow from

Does this make the user's life feel lighter, or heavier?

A value only works if it has teeth — if there's a moment we can name when honouring it costs us something. The three below have all been tested. Here's how each one behaves under pressure.

Value 01

Honest before impressive.

The honest version ships. The impressive version waits, or doesn't. When the choice is between looking good and being right, we pick right. Wabi-sabi as a daily practice, not an aesthetic.

When this gets tested
When the launch deadline is tomorrow and the product still has the rough edges. When a case study would land harder if we left out the part that didn't work. When a course module would convert better with a confidence we don't yet have.
What we do
Ship the version we'd defend in five years, even if it's less impressive today. Include the parts that didn't work. Replace performed certainty with honest uncertainty.
How we know we're holding it
If the copy could appear in a competitor's pitch deck without anyone noticing — we've drifted. If the launch felt awkward to talk about because some of it didn't land — we're holding.
Value 02

Build in public, sell in private.

The reasoning, the methods, the principles, the documents — public. The working tools, the agents, the execution kits, the curated assets — paid. The thinking is the marketing; the execution is the product.

When this gets tested
When a competitor copies a public document. When a free piece of content "should" be paywalled because it took weeks to write. When customers ask whether they need to pay for something we already published.
What we do
We let the public artefact stay public. We charge for the executable layer — the working code, the working agents, the working templates — not for the reasoning. The depth is the marketing. People pay for the wiring, not the schematic.
How we know we're holding it
If publishing the rules would expose the business — we've got it backwards. The rules aren't the moat. The execution is.
Value 03

Subtract by default.

When in doubt, we remove. Adding is the conviction-required move, not removing. Does the product get worse without it? If no, it goes.

When this gets tested
When a feature would technically work and feels like progress. When a product page is naked and it feels like it should "have more". When a course module could include "extra value" — three more lessons, four more bonuses.
What we do
We remove anything that doesn't earn its place. The course teaches what the student needs, not the catalogue we could have written. The product page sells what the product does, not the ten things it could one day do.
How we know we're holding it
If we keep adding things to look serious — we've drifted. If we keep cutting things and the product gets clearer — we're holding.

"The strength of a position comes from its consistency. Every time we make an exception, the position weakens, and decisions get slower. The values exist to make decisions faster — not by computing every trade-off, but by ruling out the ones we already know we won't take."

Goals exist to make decisions easier today. This is the picture three years out — concrete enough to test against, specific enough to disqualify the wrong opportunities. Every "should we…?" runs through this filter first.

04 Stance · what melis.ai is not

Anti-positioning is sharper than positioning.

Most brand documents tell you what a brand is. This page tells you what it isn't. When a request, opportunity, or content idea conflicts with one of these — the answer is no.

01 · Not a

Not a guru.

We don't sell certainty. We don't promise outcomes we can't deliver. We don't position any individual as someone who has figured it all out.

02 · Not a

Not a hustle-bro AI educator.

No "I made $40K with ChatGPT in one week" content. No gold gradients, no late-stage capitalism font choices, no income-reports-as-marketing.

03 · Not a

Not a thought leader.

We don't perform expertise. We don't write think-pieces about "the future of work". We don't appear on every panel about AI.

04 · Not a

Not a consultancy that doesn't ship.

We use what we sell. We run the agents we teach. If we're not actively using a tool in real work — we don't teach it.

05 · Not a

Not a lifestyle brand.

We're not selling a vibe. The papercraft imagery and dark surfaces support the work, not perform aesthetic.

06 · Not

Not enterprise.

We don't write for procurement teams. No "for enterprise" lockup. No SOC2 promises. The audience is operators, founders, individual contributors.

07 · Not a

Not a tutorial mill.

We don't churn content for SEO traffic. We don't publish "How to use Claude in 2026" listicles. The library is curated, not generated.

08 · Not

Not corporate.

No third-person About copy. No "we are pleased to announce". No press releases. No newsroom.

L The Lexicon · concepts worth carrying forward

Thirty-three concepts humanity must take forward. Things worth honouring, embedding, returning to.

English is a literal language. It does not have single words for many of the concepts other traditions encoded thousands of years ago — the right moment versus clock time, the gentle sadness at impermanence, fearless truth-telling at risk to oneself. The Lexicon is melis.ai's canon of those concepts: thirty-three terms drawn from Greek, Latin, Japanese, Sanskrit, and beyond, each one paired with a hand-coded geometric glyph. The glyph is an act of preservation; the word is the heart.

These appear throughout our surfaces — section anchors, dispatch sign-offs, course chapters, eventually physical products — as quiet reminders. Different in kind from the functional icons (Phosphor) we use for buttons and navigation. Sigils mark concepts; icons mark objects.

C The Compass · modern conceptual frames

Fifteen frames sharp enough to invoke, often. Different in kind from the Lexicon.

Where the Lexicon names concepts humanity must take forward and protect, the Compass names modern conceptual frames sharp enough to be invoked, often, in a working life. These have English names. They are not endangered. They do not need preservation. Their power is in the language. Glyphs would dilute them.

Rule: no Compass entry gets a glyph. They appear by name in dispatch, course material, essays — invoked, not illustrated.

Reasoning frames · 4

First principles

Reasoning from indivisible truths of a domain rather than analogy or convention. Aristotle's archē, Descartes's method, Musk's mantra.

Second-order thinking

Considering the consequences of consequences — what happens after what happens. The discipline of asking and then what?

Inversion

Solving by working backward from failure. Charlie Munger's signature move, descended from Carl Jacobi.

Steelmanning

Stating an opposing argument in its strongest form before disagreeing. The discipline of someone who would rather be right than win.

Decision frames · 4

Opportunity cost

Every yes is a no to everything else you could have done with the time. Saying yes is therefore an act of valuing this above all alternatives.

Sunk cost fallacy

The mistake of letting past investment determine future action. We've spent so much already is never a reason to keep going.

Asymmetric bets

Choices where the upside is large and the downside is small. Bet aggressively on these. Avoid the inverse.

Reversibility test

Before any major decision: is this reversible? If yes, decide fast and adjust. If no, slow down. Bezos's Type 1 / Type 2.

Time frames · 3

Lindy effect

A non-perishable thing's expected future life is proportional to its current age. Prefer the durable over the new.

Compounding

The most underrated force in finance, fitness, skill, relationships. Small consistent inputs, nonlinear outputs over long horizons.

Long horizon

The discipline of asking will this matter in ten years? before deciding whether it matters now. Most things won't.

Action frames · 4

Beginner's mind

Approaching a familiar thing as if for the first time. The expert's curse is that they stop seeing.

Anti-fragility

Getting stronger under stress, not just surviving it. Distinct from resilience. Taleb's contribution.

Margin of safety

Building extra room beyond what the calculation says is needed. Reality has a surprising amount of detail.

OODA loop

Observe, orient, decide, act — the speed of cycling through that loop determines who wins under uncertainty. Boyd's model.

09 Voice · how we write

Direct. Specific. Plain English. Australian. And, when it lands — funny.

We write the way a senior operator explains something to a smart colleague — neither dumbing down nor performing expertise. We trust the reader to keep up. The voice is plain by default and clever when the moment earns it. Restraint is funnier than effort. A line that makes the reader smile mid-paragraph is worth more than a paragraph that tries to.

01 · Voice

Direct.

Lead with the actual claim. Cut the warm-up. "I built Australia's first retail digital human" — not "I've been thinking about how AI changes retail and recently had the opportunity to work on something exciting…"

02 · Voice

Specific.

Concrete over conceptual. Numbers over adjectives. Names over categories. "20,000 students on Udemy" — not "trusted by thousands worldwide". "Built for L'Oréal, Maybelline, Anytime Fitness" — not "household brands across multiple verticals".

03 · Voice

Plain English.

No jargon. No SaaS-speak. No frontier-of-AI language. If a phrase sounds like it could appear in a press release, it can't appear in our copy. Words that have been worn smooth by overuse don't carry weight anymore.

04 · Voice

Australian.

Australian spelling: colour, organise, behaviour, recognise, programme, centre. Dry humour where it lands. Self-deprecation, never self-flagellation. "Yeah, nah" energy. Hero narratives are not the register.

05 · Voice

Witty — when it earns the line.

A clever analogy is worth a thousand "powerful, intuitive, game-changing"s. A cultural reference that lands tells the reader who we think they are. A throwaway joke that connects two disconnected things makes the whole paragraph come alive. But the discipline is restraint — humour earns its place; most copy doesn't need it.

06 · Voice

Story-shaped.

Even a product page has a story underneath it: here's what we tried, here's what happened, here's what we'd do differently. Reach for the texture — the specific morning, the actual cost, the conversation it came out of. Texture is what makes copy memorable.

When humour earns its place

Five signs the joke should stay in.

The brand explicitly is not edgy and straightforward all the way through — that gets monotonous. Cleverness, cultural reference, dry observation, the right pun in the right place — these are part of the brand's voice when they land. Some tests for whether they land:

  • It connects two unrelated things. A clever analogy that compares an agent system to a 19th-century clockmaker's workshop earns its place. A joke that just substitutes "AI" for "the cloud" doesn't.
  • The reference is current and accurate. A line that references the right film, the right book, the right meme of the moment — landed correctly — signals taste. Botched references signal trying-too-hard.
  • The joke survives a third read. If it's still funny on the third pass, it stays. If it gets thinner, it goes.
  • It doesn't punch down. Self-deprecation, dry observation about industries, jokes about ourselves — fine. Mocking the reader, an underdog, or a person at a disadvantage — never.
  • It earns its position. A funny line at the start of a paragraph earns a serious one at the end. Earnestness and wit work together; either one alone gets thin.

10 Imagery · tilt-shift papercraft world

The visual language is a tilt-shift papercraft world, photographed.

Miniature physical worlds of cut and folded paper. Lit warmly from inside, cool teal twilight pouring in from outside. Shot through a tilt-shift lens — sharp focal band where the figure sits, soft above and below. Frame-filling, never bounded. A small papercraft world the camera is inside, not peering at.

Why tilt-shift papercraft earned the primary slot.

Conceptual fit. "Built by hand, made of paper, lit cinematically" maps directly onto the brand's craft promise. The tilt-shift is the optical signature — the world feels miniature, the camera leans in, the figure invites you in.

Premium ceiling. Reads as Laika Studios stop-motion, Wes Anderson set, Hermès display — cultural references that pull premium without trying.

Distinctive. Almost nobody in AI-education land uses tilt-shift papercraft. Low saturation, high recognition. A language with no competitors.

The aesthetic

Six qualities. Every image earns all six.

"Tilt-shift papercraft" is the floor, not the ceiling. Below the surface description, six locked qualities make a melis.ai image distinctly ours — and rule out the ten thousand AI-generated paper-craft images that look like everything else. If a generated image misses any of these, it's regenerated.

01 · Aesthetic

Tilt-shift miniature world.

Strong, visible tilt-shift blur. The figure sits at the focal plane — sharp band in the centre, soft Gaussian blur above and below, bokeh halos where the lamplight catches. The optical effect is what creates the miniature feeling, not any visible frame.

02 · Aesthetic

Cinematic two-light tension.

Warm amber from inside the scene — a desk lamp, a hearth, the glow of a screen. Cool teal twilight pouring in from outside — a window, a doorway, a sky. The interplay between warm interior and cool exterior is the brand's lighting fingerprint. Never single-source flat lighting.

03 · Aesthetic

Frame-filling composition.

The miniature world extends to all four sides of the image. No vignette borders, no diorama-box edges, no museum-vitrine framing. The camera is inside the world, not peering at it from outside. The viewer steps in.

04 · Aesthetic

Paper grain authenticity.

Every object visibly hand-cut and folded. Crisp edges, visible paper grain, layered cardstock. Books with paper spines, plants from layered cut-paper leaves, scattered handwritten paper notes. No plastic, no chrome, no rendered surfaces. If it doesn't look like it could've been made by hand from paper, it doesn't ship.

05 · Aesthetic

Small paper figure.

A small paper figure at the focal plane is the focal subject — a hand-crafted papercraft figurine at proper scale within the room. Never a photorealistic human, never a 3D character render, never absent. The figure is who you are inside the world.

06 · Aesthetic

One magical small element.

Each scene contains one detail that doesn't quite belong — typically a small violet paper lotus glowing faintly with soft purple light. Without it, the image is a still life. With it, it's a brand asset. The lotus is the brand's visual signature — the magic, the violet anchor, the thing the eye lands on.

The blur-and-text pattern

Papercraft, blurred. Type, on top.

The same treatment used on the cover and the layer dividers — papercraft image softly blurred behind a dark gradient veil, with type set on top — is a reusable brand element. Use it whenever an image needs to support type rather than carry the frame alone. The image becomes texture; the type does the work.

Where it applies. Section headers (cover, layer dividers, course module intros). YouTube thumbnails where a headline carries the click. Substack post header cards. Carousel title slides. Course chapter cards. Long-form blog feature images where a quote sits on top. Anywhere the image is supporting evidence, not the primary subject.

The recipe. Image: papercraft scene generated via the melis-ai-paper-craft skill. Blur: 6–8px Gaussian (lighter on small surfaces, heavier on full-bleed heroes). Veil: dark gradient — heaviest where the type sits, lighter at the edges so the scene breathes through. Type: display serif (Cormorant Garamond) in --text, italic accent in --accent. Mono eyebrow above the title, optional.

The discipline. The image is a mood, not a message. If the reader needs to read the image to understand the surface, the pattern is wrong — pick a hero image instead. If the type can stand alone and the image is making it land harder, the pattern is right.

Premium ceiling — what these read like

When a melis.ai image lands, it should read as one of these references — not as "AI paper craft":

  • Laika Studios stop-motion stills — Kubo, Coraline, ParaNorman — handmade physical worlds, photographed with care.
  • Hermès window displays — small worlds lit like jewellery, telling a single story.
  • Anthology film miniatures — symmetrical sets, every prop intentional, the camera precise rather than busy.
  • Premium tilt-shift miniature photography — frame-filling, the optical effect doing the miniature work.
  • The Pudding's editorial visualisations — for the rare blueprint / schematic accent style.

If an image looks more like Pixar 3D, SaaS vector, children's-book illustration, or stock-paper-craft, regenerate. The ceiling is Laika, not library of loose stock.

The cinematic library

Variations on the recipe. Same world, different camera.

The default brand recipe is locked: tilt-shift papercraft, two-light tension, frame-filling, small paper figure, violet lotus. But a single recipe doesn't carry every story. Below: the cinematographic axes that can be deliberately varied — angle, light, treatment, setting — to extend the language without breaking it. Use these for variety, mood, and specific narrative needs. Each example below is rendered through the standard melis-ai-paper-craft skill with one parameter dialled away from the default.

The library is a vocabulary, not a brief. Reach for these when the default is too constraining. Most surfaces still use the default — it's the default for a reason.

Axis 01 · Angle

Where the camera sits.

The default is eye-level wide. These are the meaningful departures — shot size (close to wide), camera height (high to low), camera position (front, side, behind, POV) — each shift changes what the frame is about.

Axis 02 · Light

When the day is.

The default is dusk — warm interior plus cool teal twilight. The library here shifts the time of day, source, or lighting setup itself, while keeping the brand's two-light DNA wherever it can. Single-source treatments (hearth, screen, candle) are deliberate departures for intimate moods.

Axis 03 · Treatment

How the film reads.

The default is full colour with the warm/teal palette. These are the colour-grade variants — break them out for specific narrative moods. Use sparingly: monochrome, noir, and cyberpunk are striking precisely because they're not the default.

Axis 04 · Setting

Where the world lives.

The default is an interior workshop. These are the environment shifts — outdoor (nature, urban), interior beyond the studio, abstract space, and surreal dreamscapes — for when the figure needs to step out of the workshop. The papercraft language travels; the world it inhabits doesn't have to be a room.

Axis 05 · Composition

How the frame is divided.

The default is centred or naturally weighted. These are the explicit compositional grammars — symmetry, thirds, framing devices, negative space, leading lines, layered depth. Use to direct the eye and add formal structure.

Axis 06 · Atmosphere

What's in the air.

The default has subtle dust motes and slight haze. These are the explicit atmospheric elements — weather, particulate, light interaction — that lift a scene from object to moment. Atmosphere does emotional work the figure alone can't.

Axis 07 · Lens

How the world is rendered.

The default is a tilt-shift miniature lens. These are the alternative optical signatures — fisheye distortion, anamorphic widescreen, macro detail, telephoto compression, wide-angle exaggeration. Each lens choice changes the spatial feel of the world.

The tactile library

Papercraft for explanation. Diagrams, with their own labels.

The cinematic library is mood. The tactile library is meaning. Same materials — paper, thread, felt-tip, letterpress — turned to a different job: explaining things. Flow diagrams, hierarchies, matrices, charts, comparison cards. All rendered in the brand's papercraft language, with their labels generated alongside the image rather than overlaid in CSS.

Use these instead of stock infographic templates in YouTube videos, course slides, blog posts, social carousels — anywhere the standard chart-junk would feel off-brand. Each asset is captioned with its topic, so you can grab the structure and re-prompt with your own label set.

The recipe. Specify exact label text. Specify the medium — felt-tip on paper, typed paper tag, letterpress block, kraft ticket. Specify position relative to the element. Cap labels at four words; longer captions become illegible at scene scale. Close every prompt with: "all text must be sharp, legible, rendered as actual readable letters not gibberish."

The discipline. The tactile library is the deliberate exception to the "no text in images" rule. Mood images stay text-free. Tactile diagrams carry their own labels — and that's the point.

Tactile · Flow diagrams

How work moves through a system.

Boxes, arrows, paper-thread connections. Use for orchestration diagrams, decision trees, pipelines, feedback loops, fan-in/fan-out routing.

Tactile · Hierarchy

How things relate downward.

Parent above, children below. Use for org charts, brand families, value cascades, priority stacks. Tree shape with paper-thread lines.

Tactile · Frameworks & matrices

When the axes do the teaching.

2×2 and 3×3 grids with handwritten quadrant titles, axis labels, and chart titles. Use for strategy frameworks, judgment calls, maturity grids.

Tactile · Charts

How numbers sit in the world.

Stacked-paper bars and floating tag labels. Use for revenue charts, growth tiers, before/after comparisons, time-allocation breakdowns. Tag values come from the prompt — round numbers read clearer than decimals.

Tactile · Layered architecture

When the levels matter.

Stacked layers, nested boxes, pyramids, iceberg cross-sections. Use for OS-style stacks, scope diagrams, what-they-see-vs-what-it-took explanations.

Tactile · Process strips

When order is the meaning.

Numbered cards in a row, before/during/after triptychs, iteration cycles, narrowing funnels. Use for any step-by-step explanation.

Tactile · Comparison cards

When you need to contrast two things.

Two columns, paired tiles, this-vs-that splits. Use for myth-vs-truth, old-way-vs-new, amateur-vs-operator. Header label per column, list items below.

14 Influences · what we appreciate

Six lineages we draw from. None of them govern us.

Every brand has aesthetic ancestors. These are ours — six traditions of restrained, considered design that shaped how we think about typography, space, material, and the discipline of removing rather than adding. We're not minimalists. We're subtractive — and the difference matters.

"Perfection is achieved not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away."

Antoine de Saint-Exupéry · Terre des Hommes · 1939

01 · Architecture

Less is more.

Mies van der Rohe gave architecture its spine: a building can be powerful precisely because of what it withholds. Every material exposed honestly. No ornament that doesn't earn its place. Structure as beauty.

02 · Industrial design

Less, but better.

Dieter Rams' tenth principle of good design: good design is as little design as possible. Thirty years at Braun. Direct lineage to Apple. Still the clearest articulation of what good product design means.

03 · Japanese aesthetics

Kanso簡素 — simplicity

The elimination of clutter — not for efficiency, but for clarity. Removing everything that distracts from what the thing fundamentally is. A kanso tea house contains nothing that is not necessary.

04 · Japanese aesthetics

Ma間 — negative space

The intentional use of emptiness as a compositional element. The empty space is not absence — it is presence. It is where meaning lives.

05 · Japanese aesthetics

Wabi-sabi侘寂 — honest, imperfect

Accepting incompleteness and imperfection as sources of beauty. Not the polish of perfection, but the honesty of something real. Ship the honest version, not the impressive version.

06 · Practice

Subtraction as discipline.

Before adding any element we ask: does the product get worse without it? If the answer is no, it goes. Adding feels like progress. Removing requires conviction.

These shape the brand's aesthetic execution. They don't replace the manifesto, the promise, or the values — those are first-order. The lineage is second-order: how we make things, not why we make them.

Card surface system

Five tiers. Every card inherits one.

Surfaces are a brand statement, not a styling choice. The brand book uses five card tiers, each with its own job. Reach for the right tier by the role the card plays — not by what it looks like in isolation. Most cards in the brand book are tier 1 (solid). Tier 2 (heavy obsidian) is reserved for moments. Tiers 4 and 5 (image-backed) are reserved for sections where the imagery itself reinforces meaning.

Tier 1 · Solid
Default body card

When to use: data tables, palette rows, chart cards, default body containers.
Spec: background: var(--surface) + 1px var(--border-light).

Tier 2 · Obsidian heavy
Quotes, North Stars, key callouts

When to use: the moment you want the reader to stop and reread. North Stars, italic pull-quotes, the test-the-values-flow-from line.
Spec: rgba(0,0,0,0.65) + 30px backdrop blur + saturate 1.3 + top-edge inset highlight + drop shadow. Purple left-border 3px solid var(--accent).
Used on: .northstar, .quote-block.

Tier 3 · Obsidian subtle
Body card grids, secondary panels

When to use: grids of related cards where each one needs distinction without dominating. Promise principles, anti-positioning tiles, sub-brand registry items.
Spec: rgba(10,10,10,0.5) + 20px backdrop blur + saturate 1.2.
Used on: .principle.

Tier 4 · Image-backed (28px blur)
Abstract artistic glass — mood as meaning

When to use: sections where colour temperature reinforces the principle being stated. Values cards (warm/cool/minimal triad). Image is reduced to a colour wash — no specific subject visible.
Spec: per-card --scene CSS var → image as ::before at 28px blur + saturate 1.35, scaled out 40px to prevent edge bleed. Dark gradient veil ::after.
Used on: .chart-card.has-scene.

Tier 5 · Image-backed (5px blur, gradient veil)
Editorial photo + caption — image as content

When to use: sections where the image is part of the message — channel previews, sub-brand cards, anywhere the imagery should be recognisable. Image visible at top of card, gradient veil pulls darkness behind text for legibility.
Spec: per-card --scene at 5px blur + saturate 1.1. Gradient veil dark at bottom (0.85) → mid (0.55) → light at top (0.35) so type sits on the darkest band.
Reserved for: Channels section, sub-brand cards (when applied).

Choosing a tier

Default to Tier 1. Most cards should be solid. Backdrop-filter glass is real GPU cost — use it where it earns its place, not as the default.

Tier 2 is a moment. If you find yourself using it more than 2–3 times in a section, you're diluting the impact. The North Star is a moment. Every value isn't.

Tier 3 is for visual distinction in body grids. Three or four related cards that should each feel like a distinct surface, none of them dominating. Use when the felt bg makes the grid feel undifferentiated.

Tier 4 is reserved for principle sections. Values, possibly Stance. The image is reduced past recognition — what reads is the colour. Pair the colour temperature to the meaning (warm = action, cool = transmission, neutral = restraint).

Tier 5 is for sections where the image IS content. Channel previews, sub-brand cards, anywhere a recognisable scene reinforces the message. Don't use for principle statements — the image will fight the type.

Watchpoints: backdrop-filter is unreliable on iOS Safari (sometimes substituted with a static fallback) — test on real device. Image-backed cards add ~1MB per image to page weight — cache aggressively, consider WebP versions for production.

Asset library · how to use

Reference any asset by its ID — AS-14, AS-62, AS-100 — when describing pages, articles, course modules, or products. The ID is stable; the visual may evolve.

All 81 components are hand-coded HTML / CSS / SVG. No JS dependencies, no third-party libraries, no AI imagery. Copy the markup directly from this page's source. Extend the library by adding a new .asset block in the appropriate range — keep AS-XX sequential, leave gaps for siblings.

20 Do · Don't

The shortlist. Print this and pin it.

If you forget everything else, hold these. Each line is a decision rule that has cost something to keep — and is worth what it cost.

Do

  • Strip everything you can. If it's removable without loss, remove it.
  • Use mono eyebrows everywhere — they are the brand's structural fingerprint.
  • Trust empty space. Let the page breathe.
  • Treat the reader as a peer. Concrete over conceptual.
  • Use Australian spelling: colour, organise, behaviour, recognise.
  • Render italic display in accent purple — emphasis without weight.
  • Pair amber + teal in imagery. Always two-light.
  • Ship the honest version, not the impressive version.

Don't

  • Don't use accent purple for decoration. Purple has a job.
  • Don't add drop shadows for depth. Use tone shifts.
  • Don't extend the palette. If you need a new colour, you're solving the wrong problem.
  • Don't write anything you wouldn't say to a peer you respect.
  • Don't mix more than two image styles on one page.
  • Don't put body copy inside mood imagery — let the type sit on top via blur-and-text. The tactile library is the deliberate exception: those labels are the point.
  • Don't imitate Stripe / Linear / Vercel. Their language is theirs. Earn yours.
  • Don't use any word from the banlist. Ever.
21 Closing · where this brand is now

Honest before impressive — including about the brand itself.

A brand book like this one risks reading as if everything is solved. It isn't. melis.ai is one operator's working theory of how a small AI-education brand should look, sound, and behave at this moment in the field — codified in detail because consistency at small scale needs more rigour than at large scale, not less.

What's true here: the values, the stance, the visual language, the typography stack, the data-viz discipline, the tactile and cinematic libraries. These have all been tested in real work. They survive contact with shipping.

What's still settling: the website rebuild is in flight. The Soul of the Machine newsletter has the look but the cadence is being established. The YouTube essay grammar is clear in description but the proof will come video by video. The sub-brand registry will move as products ship and others sunset. The component library expands as we hit new patterns.

This document is the spine, not the finished body. If a future decision contradicts a rule in this book, the rule changes — and the change shows up here, with the date and a line on why. That's the contract. Living document, not stone tablet.

If a brand has to keep secrets to stay impressive, it's already over.
This brand book is the first artefact that can't keep one.
